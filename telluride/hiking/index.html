Telluride Hiking

Waterfalls, alpine lakes, and 14,000-foot peaks

Telluride's box canyon setting means world-class hiking starts right from town. No driving required for trails to waterfalls, alpine lakes, and ridgeline views. The elevation (8,750 feet in town) demands respect, but rewards with scenery that rivals anything in the Rockies. Prime hiking season runs late June through September.

Easy to Moderate Trails

Accessible from town, stunning payoffs

EASY

Bear Creek Trail

2.4 mi | 1,040 ft gain | Easy-Moderate
Telluride's most popular hike. Starts at the south end of Pine Street and climbs through aspen forest to 80-foot Bear Creek Falls. Allow 2-3 hours round trip.

MOD

Jud Wiebe Trail

3 mi loop | 1,300 ft gain | Moderate
The classic Telluride loop with panoramic town and box canyon views. Starts from Tomboy Road, loops through aspen and evergreen forest. Best morning hike.

MOD

Bridal Veil Falls Road

3.8 mi RT | 1,200 ft gain | Moderate
Hike up the 4WD road to Colorado's tallest free-falling waterfall (365 feet). The historic powerhouse at the top still generates electricity. Steep but wide gravel road.

MOD

Cornet Creek Falls

1.5 mi RT | 500 ft gain | Easy-Moderate
Quick hike to a hidden waterfall. Trailhead at north end of Aspen Street. Less crowded than Bear Creek with a nice cascade reward.

Challenging Hikes

Alpine lakes and high peaks

HARD

Blue Lake

6 mi RT | 2,200 ft gain | Challenging
Stunning alpine lake at 11,000 feet beneath Gilpin Peak. Starts from Bridal Veil Falls road. The final push is steep but the turquoise lake payoff is worth it.

HARD

Hope Lake

6.5 mi RT | 2,600 ft gain | Challenging
High alpine lake accessible from Trout Lake. One of the most photogenic lakes in the San Juans with dramatic peak reflections. Start early.

HARD

Bear Creek to Wasatch

11 mi RT | 3,800 ft gain | Strenuous
Extend Bear Creek Falls trail to La Junta Basin and Wasatch Trail. Full-day adventure with wildflower meadows and ridgeline views.

14ER

Wilson Peak

12+ mi RT | 4,300 ft gain | Expert
The iconic 14,017-foot peak visible from Telluride. Technical Class 3 scrambling required. Start before dawn. The Coors logo mountain.

Hiking Tips

What you need to know

When is hiking season in Telluride?

Most trails are snow-free from late June through September. Lower trails (Bear Creek, Jud Wiebe) open earlier. Alpine lakes often hold snow into July. Fall colors peak late September.

How do I handle the altitude?

Telluride sits at 8,750 feet. Arrive a day early if possible, hydrate constantly, and take it slower than normal. Start with easier trails before attempting alpine hikes.

Are there bears in Telluride?

Black bears are common. Keep food secured, make noise on trails, and carry bear spray for backcountry hikes. Bears are most active dawn and dusk.

What about afternoon thunderstorms?

Summer thunderstorms roll in almost daily, usually 1-3pm. Start hikes early and plan to be off exposed ridges by noon. Lightning at altitude is serious.
