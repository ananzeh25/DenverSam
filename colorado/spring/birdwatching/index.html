Spring Birdwatching

Migration brings the skies alive

Colorado's Migratory Crossroads

Where mountain and prairie species meet

Spring migration transforms Colorado into a birdwatcher's paradise. The state sits at the intersection of major flyways, bringing species from Central America, Mexico, and the Gulf Coast through on their way to northern breeding grounds. Over 500 species have been recorded in Colorado.

The diversity of habitats - from prairie to alpine tundra - means incredible variety within short distances. In a single day, you might spot sandhill cranes on the plains, mountain bluebirds in the foothills, and ptarmigan above treeline. Few places offer such range.

Peak spring migration runs March through May, with different species peaking at different times. Waterfowl come through early. Warblers and songbirds peak in May. Raptors ride thermals throughout. The Front Range's reservoirs and wetlands concentrate migrating birds into accessible viewing areas.

Best Birding Spots

BARR

Barr Lake State Park

Premier birding destination near Denver. 350+ species recorded. Bald eagle nest. Boardwalk through wetlands. Nature center with programs.

ARSENAL

Rocky Mountain Arsenal

Urban wildlife refuge minutes from downtown Denver. Bison, deer, and 330+ bird species. Driving loop and hiking trails. Free admission.

CHATFIELD

Chatfield State Park

Reservoir and wetlands attract waterfowl, shorebirds, and raptors. Herons rookery. Multiple habitats in one park. South Denver location.

PAWNEE

Pawnee Grasslands

Prairie species you won't find elsewhere. Mountain plovers, burrowing owls, lark buntings. Two hours northeast of Denver. Remote and wild.

Spring Birding Tips

EARLY

Go Early

Dawn is prime time. Birds are most active and vocal in early morning. Wind picks up later, making spotting difficult.

GEAR

Bring Binoculars

8x or 10x magnification works for most situations. Spotting scopes help for distant waterfowl. Field guides or apps for identification.

LAYER

Dress for Weather

Spring mornings are cold, afternoons warm. Layers essential. Waterproof boots for wetland areas. Wind protection important.

REPORT

Check eBird

Real-time sightings from other birders. Know what's being seen where. Submit your own sightings to help the community.
